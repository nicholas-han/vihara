# Instrument Manager

The instrument manager is the registry for assets, tradable instruments, reference instruments, instrument families, venue listings, and relationships between them.

It should not be only a symbol table. Its purpose is to describe how financial products are classified, generated, traded, risk-managed, settled, and mapped to external venues.

## Core Concepts

### Asset Class

An asset class is a high-level economic classification, such as equity, fixed income, commodity, currency, crypto, volatility, event, fund, or real world asset.

Asset class is useful for reporting, risk grouping, product discovery, permissions, and downstream analytics. It should not be used as a substitute for an instrument relationship graph.

Asset classes can form a hierarchy through a parent asset class. Broad grouping classes can be marked as not assignable so assets cannot be directly classified at too generic a level. For example, `EQUITY` can be non-assignable while `COMMON_STOCK` is assignable, so `TSLA` should be classified as `COMMON_STOCK` rather than directly as `EQUITY`.

### Asset

An asset is an economic object or reference object, such as BTC, ETH, USD, USDC, AAPL, SPX, a political event, a treasury bill, or a vault strategy.

Some assets are directly transferable or ownable. Others are only references used by indices, derivatives, prediction markets, or structured products.

### Instrument Type

An instrument type describes the contract form, such as spot, index, perpetual, future, option, swap, forward, fund share, prediction outcome, synthetic product, or structured product.

Instrument type affects required fields, lifecycle rules, trading rules, risk calculations, settlement, and accounting.

Instrument types should remain a flat list in the core schema. More specific terms such as call, put, European exercise, barrier features, delivery rules, or funding formulas should live on instrument families, concrete instruments, or future type-specific terms tables rather than as instrument type subtypes.

### Instrument Family

An instrument family describes a group of instruments generated by a shared product template. It is a contract definition concept, not a broad risk aggregation bucket.

Examples:

- S&P 500 index references.
- E-mini S&P 500 futures.
- E-mini S&P 500 options on futures.
- BTC-USD perpetuals.
- Hyperliquid HIP-3 markets.
- Tokenized treasury bill products.

Families should store shared product rules, such as underlying selection, expiry cycle, contract multiplier, settlement style, exercise style, quote asset, settlement asset, symbol convention, and margin convention.

Instrument families should stay relatively narrow. For example, `SPX_INDEX_OPTIONS`, `EMINI_SP500_FUTURES`, `EMINI_SP500_OPTIONS_ON_FUTURES`, `SPY_ETF`, and `SPY_OPTIONS` should be separate instrument families even though they may share similar S&P 500 market exposure.

### Instrument

An instrument is a concrete financial product or reference product.

Examples:

- `.SPX` index.
- `ESM2026` future.
- `ESM2026_C_6000` option.
- `BTC_USD_PERP`.
- `AAPL`.
- `AAPL_20260619_C_250`.
- `VAULT_SHARE_XYZ`.
- `TRUMP_2028_YES`.

An instrument may be tradable or non-tradable. Non-tradable instruments are still important because they can be underlyings, indices, oracle references, settlement references, or reporting references.

### Venue Instrument

A venue instrument maps an internal instrument to a concrete venue listing.

Examples:

- Internal `BTC_USD_PERP` mapped to Hyperliquid symbol `BTC`.
- Internal `BTC_USD_PERP` mapped to Binance symbol `BTCUSDT`.
- Internal `ESM2026` mapped to a CME symbol.
- Internal HIP-3 market mapped to a Hyperliquid deployed market id.

Venue instruments hold venue-specific rules such as venue symbol, tick size, lot size, minimum order size, order types, maker/taker fee rules, status, margin mode, and trading permissions.

### Risk Underlying Group

A risk underlying group is a portfolio and risk aggregation concept. It groups instruments or instrument families that share the same or similar economic exposure.

Examples:

- `US_EQUITY_SP500`: SPX index, SPX options, E-mini S&P 500 futures, options on E-mini futures, SPY, and SPY options.
- `CRYPTO_BTC`: BTC spot, BTC perpetuals, BTC futures, BTC options, and BTC-linked structured products.

Risk underlying groups should be used for delta aggregation, scenario risk, stress testing, hedge views, and portfolio exposure reports. They should not replace instrument families because products inside one risk group can have very different contract terms, settlement rules, multipliers, venues, and lifecycles.

## Relationship Graph

Instrument relationships should be modeled explicitly rather than embedded only as a single `underlying_id` field.

Important relationship types include:

- `UNDERLYING`: the direct economic underlying.
- `DERIVATIVE_OF`: a broader derivative dependency.
- `SETTLES_TO`: the instrument or asset delivered or paid on settlement.
- `TRACKS`: an instrument tracks an index, NAV, oracle, or benchmark.
- `INDEX_CONSTITUENT`: an asset or instrument is part of an index.
- `BASKET_CONSTITUENT`: an asset or instrument is part of a basket.
- `COLLATERAL_ASSET`: an asset can be used as collateral.
- `ORACLE_SOURCE`: a source used for marking or settlement.
- `CONVERTS_TO`: an instrument converts into another asset or instrument.

Relationships should support effective dates and metadata because product definitions can change over time.

## Recommended V0 Scope

The first version should support the Hyperliquid builder business while preserving room for futures, options, RWA, prediction markets, and exchange infrastructure.

V0 should include:

- Asset class registry.
- Asset registry.
- Instrument type registry.
- Instrument family registry.
- Instrument registry.
- Instrument relationship table.
- Venue registry.
- Venue instrument mapping.
- Risk underlying groups.
- Risk underlying group members.
- Basic status and lifecycle fields.
- JSON metadata for type-specific fields.

PostgreSQL should be the system of record. JSONB can be used for product-specific fields, but core identifiers and relationship fields should remain structured.

## Design Rules

- Asset class is classification; underlying is a relationship.
- Assets should point to the most specific assignable asset class available, not a broad grouping class.
- Instrument type is contract form; instrument family is product template.
- Risk underlying group is the broad exposure bucket for portfolio and risk views.
- Instrument is the internal product identity; venue instrument is the external trading identity.
- Derived state should refer to stable internal instrument ids, not raw venue symbols.
- Reference instruments such as indices and oracle marks should be first-class instruments even when they are not directly tradable.
- Instrument definitions should be versionable through status, effective dates, metadata, and relationship history.
